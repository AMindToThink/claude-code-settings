## 1. The auditability claim is overstated in a fixable way

The proposal says the disciple's learned content is "wholly readable and auditable as pieces of advice." It is not, and the reason is mechanical. The distillation target is not the advice — it is the teacher's full token-level distribution $P(\cdot \mid \text{context}, \text{advice})$. The advice is a *trigger* that reshapes that distribution; the distribution is what the student actually matches. Three words of advice ("consider edge cases") induce a high-dimensional behavioral change those three words do not transparently specify. You can read the advice, but you cannot read off *what competence it transferred*. The advice is a sufficient handle, not a faithful description.

This also punctures the MONA analogy in the same spot. The proposal argues the disciple's training is myopic (per-token match), so all non-myopic structure must come from readable advice. But the teacher's distribution is autoregressive and already contains lookahead — multi-step planning that the advice merely elicited rather than spelled out. The loss is myopic; the *target* is not. So "multi-step strategies must originate from advice humans can read" is too strong$_{85\%}$.

**The fix.** Weaken the claim to something still valuable and now testable. The honest version is that the advice is a **necessary, legible bottleneck** on transfer — not a complete mechanistic description of it. If ablating or paraphrasing the advice destroys the capability gain, then monitoring the channel gives a legible veto over what gets transferred, even without a full readout. Vetoing "hardcode the tests" is useful even if you cannot predict everything each piece of advice does.

This makes the **causal-necessity ablation** the spine of the project, present from day one rather than deferred. Train the disciple with (a) real advice, (b) paraphrased advice, (c) ablated/shuffled advice, (d) length-matched irrelevant advice. The auditability claim lives or dies on whether the capability gain tracks the legible content and vanishes without it.

## 2. A cleaner reframe — the ICL-ceiling bound

Teacher and student share weights; the autodidact only emits a string. So the distillation target is bounded by **what the disciple's own weights can express in-context given the best possible advice** — the disciple's in-context-learning (ICL) ceiling. The autodidact never injects its own distribution, only a prompt.

Consequence — a single distillation step cannot transfer anything the disciple could not already do in-context. The "nearly uncapped performance" comes entirely from *iteration*. Each round raises the ceiling, so the next round's advice can elicit more. Per-step you are amortizing in-context skill into weights (Snell-style context distillation, which the proposal already cites); the uncapping is a ratchet across rounds.

Worth adopting explicitly because it locates where the method *structurally cannot reach*. Skills the autodidact learned via RL but that are not elicitable-by-description in the disciple will not transfer — precisely the "alien concepts" in the proposal's notes. The gap between "elicitable by advice" and "all RL-learned skills" is the un-auditable remainder, and **characterizing that gap is itself a contribution**, arguably more interesting than the positive result.

## 3. The faithfulness gap — usefulness ≠ fidelity

The proposal's stated assumption — if the autodidact knows reward hacking works and is trained to give good advice, it will say it — assumes advice usefulness implies advice fidelity. But the autodidact's advice is selected for *helping the disciple*, not for *describing the autodidact's own policy*. These can diverge. The autodidact can help the disciple via a different mechanism than the one driving its own success, or convey a strategy implicitly.

So the channel audits "what advice helps the disciple," which is related to but not identical to "what the autodidact learned." Reward hacking surfaces only if articulable reward-hacking advice is the most effective advice. Plausible, but it is the CoT-faithfulness problem wearing a different hat, and should be treated as an empirical question rather than an assumption. (See §6 for why the closest existing measurement — Chen et al. 2025 — points the wrong way for this assumption.)

## 4. Decouple the nested optimization

Joint optimization — outer RL on the autodidact, inner distillation of a moving disciple, evaluation of that disciple — gives a non-stationary reward (the same advice has different value as the disciple changes) and a brutal per-step cost. Split it.

- **Advice optimization.** Reward the autodidact for advice that raises the disciple's *in-context* pass rate against a frozen disciple. This is RL-based prompt optimization — stationary, cheap, debuggable. (The proposal's "one-reward signal" version.)

- **Context distillation.** Separately distill the in-context gains into disciple weights, with the advice policy frozen.

- **Alternate** (1) and (2) at coarse granularity rather than nesting them.

This is also closer to how the surrounding literature actually gets things to converge.

## 5. "Distillation" is doing two jobs — keep them separate

There are two distinct operations in the pipeline, and the word "distillation" slides between them in a way that hides the central risk.

- **Autodidact → advice.** The autodidact compresses what it learned into a short natural-language artifact.

- **Advice → disciple weights.** The advice-conditioned teacher's distribution is matched by the student. This is distillation in the standard technical sense.

Operation 2 is knowledge distillation as the field uses the term. Operation 1 is the thing the auditability claim rests on, and it is **not** any of the distillation procedures in the report. The distinction that matters — every distillation method in the report produces an artifact in the *same modality as the training signal*, by an optimization that *guarantees a fidelity relationship* to the source. Dataset distillation minimizes a loss tying $\mathcal{S}$ to $\mathcal{D}$'s gradient or trajectory. KD minimizes a divergence tying student logits to teacher logits. Fidelity is enforced by the objective.

Operation 1 has no such tie. The advice is selected for *downstream usefulness to the disciple*, not for *fidelity to the autodidact's policy*. Nothing in the objective forces the advice to be a faithful compression of what the autodidact learned. So calling it "distillation" imports a connotation — "this artifact provably reflects the source" — that the mechanism does not earn. **The distillation literature's artifacts are faithful by construction; the advice is useful by construction, and useful ≠ faithful.**

The closer technical match for operation 1 is **rationale distillation / articulation** — STaR, Distilling Step-by-Step, out-of-context reasoning (Treutlein et al.) — where a model emits a verbalization of its reasoning. The field's verdict on whether those verbalizations are faithful is the report's §6 — mostly not. So the literature on the thing you are actually doing already says the artifact tends to be unfaithful. Not fatal — it is the central empirical question — but the word "distillation" smuggles in the conclusion that needs proving.

**The useful version of the intuition.** Distillation methods earn fidelity via a loss tying artifact to source. The advice currently has only a usefulness loss. If you *also* require the advice to be causally necessary and sufficient for the disciple's gain — the §1 ablation — you install the missing fidelity constraint at the behavioral level rather than the distributional level. The advice then becomes a faithful distillation of *the transferable part of what the autodidact learned*, where "transferable" is the disciple's elicitability ceiling (§2). That is a defensible, narrow, true claim, structurally analogous to how DD's fidelity holds only *up to* the inductive biases of its matching procedure. So — keep the framing, but the advice is a *candidate* distillation, and the ablation is the fidelity test that decides whether it earns the name.

## 6. Connections to the data distillation report

Most of the report is method-irrelevant — bilevel dataset distillation solves a different problem (compress a dataset, not route capability through language). But three threads bear directly on this project, as *threat model and external validation* rather than as method.

### 6.1 Your method is on-policy self-distillation (report §2), and the divergence choice is a second leak

The disciple loop *is* the GKD/MiniLLM mechanism. The §2 finding that matters — the distillation loss determines *which part of the teacher distribution the student matches*. With $D_{\mathrm{JSD}(\beta)}$,

$$D_{\mathrm{JSD}(\beta)}(p \,\|\, q) = \beta\,\mathrm{KL}(p\,\|\,M) + (1-\beta)\,\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,M), \quad M = \beta p + (1-\beta)q,$$

$\beta \to 1$ is forward KL (mode-covering), $\beta \to 0$ is reverse KL (mode-seeking). If the advice-conditioned teacher places mass on several strategies and you train under reverse KL, the student collapses onto one sharp mode — and *which* mode is not something the advice text tells you. You would be auditing the advice while the divergence silently selects among the behaviors that advice licensed. That is a leak beyond the §1 one, and it is tunable. The "thinkish ↔ performance" parameter is, I suspect$_{70\%}$, partly a $\beta$ knob in disguise. Make explicit which divergence you use, and measure whether the transferred behavior is divergence-sensitive.

### 6.2 The three §8 negative results are direct threats to the central claim

- **Subliminal learning** (Cloud et al. 2025). Same-base teacher/student transmit traits through semantically unrelated, filtered data. The disciple's teacher and student *share weights* — exactly the same-base condition. A misaligned trait in the disciple's own weights could be reinforced through the distillation step **without ever appearing in the advice**, because transmission rides on shared initialization, not text. The "different initializations for autodidact and disciple" idea defends the autodidact→disciple hop against steganography, but the disciple's teacher and student are still the same model, so the subliminal channel is wide open inside the distillation step itself. This is an argument for different teacher/student initializations too — though that breaks the self-distillation setup, so there is real tension to resolve. *(The single most important connection.)*

- **CoT unfaithfulness / silent reward hacks** (Chen et al. 2025). Reward hacks exploited at near-100% while verbalized <20%. This is the empirical reason to doubt the proposal's assumption that a hack-knowing autodidact trained for good advice will *say* "hardcode the tests." Usefulness did not imply verbalization in the closest existing measurement. (§3 is the same point; this is its citation.)

- **Persistence through distillation** (Hubinger et al. 2024). Backdoors survived having their CoT scaffolding distilled away. A *clean* advice channel does not guarantee a clean disciple — weight-level behavior can persist through the distillation step independent of the text.

### 6.3 Influence functions (report §4) as the external check

The method asserts the advice channel captures what transferred; influence functions / training-data attribution (EK-FAC, the Anthropic work) test that from the weight side. Attribute a disciple behavior back to training signal and see whether it lands on advice-conditioned distillation steps or somewhere else. The report's §8 recommendation was triangulate, never trust one tool. The advice channel is one leg; IF attribution is the leg that could catch what the channel misses.

**Sharper framing of the contribution.** The report's throughline is that every route from "what the model learned" to a readable artifact passes through a lossy projection. The language bottleneck is an attempt to beat that. So the value of the project is partly that it is a *test* of whether a forced-articulation bottleneck leaks less than the alternatives. The honest expectation, given §6 and §7 of the report, is that it leaks but less — and the interesting result is *quantifying the residual*, i.e. the gap between what the autodidact learned and what the faithful, auditable advice can carry. Nobody has quantified that for a forced-language-bottleneck setup.

## 7. Relevant work not cited in the proposal

Pre-cutoff items I am fairly confident about. (The 2601/2602 papers in the proposal's related-work section are at or past my knowledge boundary, so I am taking those descriptions as given.)

- **Emergent communication / referential games** (Lazaridou et al. 2017+, Havrylov & Titov 2017). The most important omission. An optimized message channel between two networks, scored only by a downstream task, reliably drifts toward non-compositional, non-human-legible codes. A direct prediction of the central failure mode, and a source of mitigations (legibility rewards, population/anti-stego pressure). The different-initialization idea blocks naive shared-codebook stego but not autodidact-specific encodings tuned to this disciple. An independent-reader legibility check should be in the loop from the start, not in "further experiments."

- **Deep Language Networks** (Sordoni et al. 2023, "Joint Prompt Optimization of Stacked LLMs"). Stacked LLMs communicating in natural language, optimized end-to-end. Structurally very close to the autodidact→disciple channel; relevant for both method and the legibility-vs-performance tradeoff.

- **Iterated Amplification / Distillation** (Christiano et al. 2018) and **Debate**. The loop — autodidact improves, transmits to disciple, repeat — is an amplify-then-distill cycle. Worth situating against IDA's known failure modes.

- **Eliciting Latent Knowledge** (Christiano / ARC 2021) as the framing. The proposal is a specific mechanism for ELK via a forced language bottleneck. Naming the connection sharpens the contribution.

- **Distilling System 2 into System 1** (Yu, Weston et al. 2024). Amortizing in-context reasoning into weights — step (2) of §4 under a different name.

- **Out-of-context reasoning / "Connecting the Dots"** (Treutlein et al. 2024) and articulation studies. Bear directly on whether a model *can* verbalize what it learned, which is the faithfulness precondition.

- **Machine teaching** (Zhu et al.) as the formal frame for "find the message that best teaches the receiver." The autodidact is a learned teacher in this sense.

## 8. Tractability risks, ordered

- Legibility collapse under optimization pressure (the emergent-comms prediction). Mitigate early with paraphrase defense + independent-reader legibility reward.

- Advice fidelity ≠ advice usefulness (§3).

- Non-stationary / expensive reward if you nest rather than decouple (§4).

- ICL-ceiling bound per step (§2); uncapping requires the ratchet to actually climb, which must be demonstrated, not assumed.

- Standard RL instability, already noted in the proposal.

## 9. Suggested experiment order

- Run the advice-transfer MVP (does RL transfer to advice-giving).

- The decoupled advice-optimization + context-distillation loop on a small RLVR task (§4).

- The causal-necessity ablation as the headline auditability result (§1).

- The reward-hacking environment.

- The steganography / legibility stress test (with an independent reader).

- Characterization of the un-transferable remainder (§2).

## 10. One open question that changes the success criterion

When the proposal says the disciple should end up "much stronger," does it mean stronger than its own starting point (the ratchet, which the architecture supports) or approaching the autodidact (bounded by elicitability, and may not hold)? Those imply different success criteria and different experiments — worth pinning down before committing to a headline metric.
