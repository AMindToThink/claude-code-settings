\author{Matthew Khoriaty}

\begin{abstract}
An autoregressive language model is trained to \emph{predict} text, so when it
\emph{generates} it has an incentive to model its own \emph{decoder} --- the
temperature, top-$k$ cutoff, or banned-token list that turns its logits into
tokens. Does the model know that decoder, and can we read it off the model's own
logits, training-free? We report preliminary steps, in three beats.
\textbf{(1)}~The model tracks its decoder: ban a token or impose a top-$k$ cutoff
while it generates, and the model --- reading back its own constrained text ---
lowers the right probabilities. \textbf{(2)}~A streaming estimator reads an
effective inverse-temperature $\beta = 1/T$ off the prefix one token at a time;
on known-temperature self-text it recovers the true $1/T$ and predicts
near-optimally, though natural text carries little to recalibrate (Qwen-2.5-3B is
temperature-robust). \textbf{(3)}~Our central result is a \textbf{partial proof}:
building that estimator as a grid (``bucket'') of fixed-temperature experts makes
it exactly Vovk's Aggregating Algorithm for log loss, and it therefore carries
two \emph{per-sequence, distribution-free} guarantees on arbitrary streams --- a
forecast-regret bound of $\ln G$ against the best bucket, and a convex level-set
error bar around the grid MLE (Lemma~\ref{lem:certificate}) --- while certifying
the Beat-2 read-out. Turning the read-out into better \emph{generation}
self-neutralizes and is left open. All numbers are script-generated and cited by
name.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Does the model know its own decoder?}\label{sec:know}
% =====================================================================
At each step an autoregressive language model emits a distribution over next
tokens; \emph{decoding} chooses how to sample from it.
Because models are trained to predict text rather than to generate it, a model
that reads back its own generated prefix has an incentive to infer the decoder
that produced it and adjust its predictions accordingly \citep{basu2021mirostat}.
The question this project addresses is whether that decoder --- concretely, an
effective inverse-temperature $\beta = 1/T$ --- can be \emph{read off the model's
own logits online, training-free, and with guarantees}, and whether the read-out
is useful for prediction or generation.
This sits at the intersection of post-hoc calibration
\citep{guo2017calibration,liu2024temperature} and the
algorithms-with-predictions viewpoint \citep{mitzenmacher2020algorithms} of the
course, in which a possibly-unreliable prediction augments a downstream decision;
what we then most want is a \emph{worst-case, per-sequence} guarantee that holds
even when the stream is misspecified, which Section~\ref{sec:cert} delivers.

\paragraph{The model tracks its decoder.}
We constrain the decoder while the model generates, then ask whether the model,
reading back its own constrained text, has inferred the constraint.
\textbf{Blacklist:} ban a token during generation; the model lowers its predicted
probability on that token across the sequence
($\Delta p_{\text{late--early}} = \blRankOneDeltaP$ for the top token, and below
an unbanned control $\blRankOneTvc$, 95\% CI $[\blRankOneTvcLo,\blRankOneTvcHi]$),
with an early-position contrast $\approx 0$ as a sanity check
(Figure~\ref{fig:blacklist}).
\textbf{Top-$k$:} truncating to the top $k$ produces no significant cliff at $k$
itself; instead probability concentrates on the head, the more so the tighter the
cutoff.
So the model does track its decoder --- though the top-$k$ signal is broad
sharpening rather than a $k$-specific edge, a conservative reading the controls do
not let us strengthen to ``infers the exact constraint.''

\section{A streaming estimator for the effective temperature}\label{sec:filter}
% =====================================================================
If the model tracks its decoder, can we make that quantitative and \emph{useful}?
A streaming \emph{filter} reads an inverse-temperature $\hat\beta_t = 1/\hat T_t$
off the prefix one token at a time, training-free, using only the model's own
logits.%
\footnote{A prior-regularized one-step online MLE on $\eta=\log\beta$: with a
log-normal prior (mean $1$, scale $\sigma_0$) and a per-token Newton/Fisher step
of the categorical likelihood under $q=\mathrm{softmax}(\ell_t)$, using
$\mathrm{score}_t=\ell_t[x_t]-\mathbb{E}_q[\ell_t]$ and
$J_t=\sigma_0^{-2}+\sum_{\tau\le t}\mathrm{Var}_q[\ell_\tau]$ evaluated at
$\beta{=}1$. Implementation and unit tests (shift invariance,
batched\,$=$\,serial, recovery under matched-prior synthetic data):
\texttt{src/decoding\_decoding/counter\_decode.py}.}
\textbf{Recovery:} on Qwen self-text generated at a \emph{known} temperature
($\scNseq$ sequences per $T$), $\hat\beta_t$ settles on the true $\log(1/T)$ from
the prefix alone (bias $\scFaithBiasZeroSeven$ at $T{=}0.7$;
Figure~\ref{fig:selfcal}, left).
\textbf{Prediction:} using $\hat\beta_t$ as a per-token temperature beats the
default $\beta{=}1$ model by $\scNllGainZeroSeven$ nats at $T{=}0.7$ (up to
$\scNllGainTwo$ at $T{=}2$), within $\scOracleGapTwo$ nats of an oracle that
\emph{knows} $1/T$ (Figure~\ref{fig:selfcal}, right).
Because the filter uses only information the model itself has, it is something the
model could in principle run internally to predict better.
But does \emph{natural} text carry a recoverable temperature? Fitting the best
global temperature per text type over $\scSurvNDatasets$ types from
in-distribution to far-OOD (appendix Table~\ref{tab:survey}), Qwen is
temperature-robust everywhere: optimal $T\in[\scSurvOptTRecaman,\scSurvMaxOptT]$
and the best temperature buys $\le\scSurvGainRecaman$ nats on any real type
($\scSurvGainShuffledWikitext$ only on shuffled gibberish).
So the read-out is real and usable on temperatured text, but in the wild there is
essentially nothing to recalibrate.

\paragraph{What the filter lacks.}
This filter is fast, but its experts are \emph{not} fixed (it re-linearizes the
likelihood at $\beta{=}1$ each step), so it carries \emph{no} per-sequence
guarantee: on an adversarial or misspecified stream nothing bounds how wrong
$\hat\beta_t$ can be. Supplying exactly that guarantee --- and certifying this
filter's outputs against it --- is the subject of the next section.

\section{A streaming estimator \emph{with guarantees}: the bucket certificate}\label{sec:cert}
% =====================================================================
Fix a grid (a set of ``buckets'') of inverse-temperatures
$\beta_1 < \dots < \beta_G$, log-spaced over a bounded range. Treat each
$\beta_g$ as a fixed \emph{expert} that forecasts the next token with probability
$P_g(x_t) = \mathrm{softmax}(\beta_g\,\ell_t)[x_t]$ on the model's \emph{raw}
logits $\ell_t \in \mathbb{R}^V$ (no rescaling --- fixed experts are what make
the guarantees hold). Let
\[
  L_n(\beta) = \sum_{t\le n} \big[\mathrm{lse}(\beta\ell_t) - \beta\,\ell_t[x_t]\big],
  \qquad \mathrm{lse}(\cdot) = \log\textstyle\sum_v \exp(\cdot),
\]
be the cumulative log loss of bucket $\beta$, let $\pi_0$ be a prior on the grid,
and let the streaming forecaster predict $x_t$ with probability
$\sum_g w_g(t)\,P_g(x_t)$, where $w_g(t) \propto \pi_0[g]\,e^{-L_{t-1}(\beta_g)}$
is the Bayes posterior over buckets. This is precisely Vovk's Aggregating
Algorithm for log loss \citep{vovk1998game,cesabianchi2006prediction}, and the
following holds for \emph{every} logit/token sequence --- with no assumption that
tokens were sampled from any $P_\beta$, or from anything at all.

\begin{lemma}[per-sequence certificate]\label{lem:certificate}
For any logit sequence $\ell_{1:n}$ and any token sequence $x_{1:n}$:
(i)~the forecaster's cumulative log loss is
$-\ln \sum_g \pi_0[g]\, e^{-L_n(\beta_g)} \le L_n(\beta_g) + \ln(1/\pi_0[g])$
for every $g$; with uniform $\pi_0$ the regret against the best bucket in
hindsight is at most $\ln G$.
(ii)~With uniform $\pi_0$ the posterior mode equals $\arg\min_g L_n(\beta_g)$,
the best-fit (\emph{effective}) inverse temperature on the grid, exactly.
(iii)~$L_n$ is convex with $L_n''(\beta) = \sum_t \mathrm{Var}_{q_{\beta,t}}[\ell_t]$,
$q_{\beta,t} = \mathrm{softmax}(\beta\ell_t)$, so for any $c>0$ the sub-level set
$\{\beta : L_n(\beta) \le \min L_n + c\}$ is an interval containing the sequence
MLE; its endpoints, read off the stored grid loss profile, are a data-dependent
error bar that needs no distributional assumptions.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
(i)~The forecaster's per-step log probabilities telescope:
\[
  \textstyle\sum_t \ln \sum_g w_g(t) P_g(x_t)
  = \ln \sum_g \pi_0[g]\, e^{-L_n(\beta_g)}
  \ge \ln\big(\pi_0[g]\, e^{-L_n(\beta_g)}\big)\ \text{for each } g.
\]
(ii)~Immediate from $w_g(n) \propto e^{-L_n(\beta_g)}$.
(iii)~Each summand of $L_n$ is an $\mathrm{lse}$ of an affine function of $\beta$
minus an affine function, hence convex; differentiate twice.
\end{proof}

``Distribution-free'' here does double duty. Over the data process, the lemma
assumes nothing about how $x_{1:n}$ was produced. Over the estimator's own
assumptions, with uniform $\pi_0$ the mode in (ii) \emph{is} the grid MLE and the
bar in (iii) is an exact profile-likelihood interval (not a Gaussian
approximation); the prior survives only as the constant $c=\ln G$. We report the
mode, not the posterior mean: by (ii) it equals the best-fit bucket with zero
slack, whereas the mean of the (unimodal but skewed --- $e^{-L_n}$ is log-concave
by (iii)) posterior over a log-spaced grid is mildly biased.

\paragraph{Three honest caveats.}
First, the certificate is relative to the in-hindsight best-fit $\beta$, not the
dial a counterparty actually set; under a misspecified decoder (a top-$k$ cutoff,
a blacklist) no single ``true'' $\beta$ describes the stream, and the best-fit
value is exactly what ``effective temperature'' should mean there. The bar then
certifies \emph{fit to the observed stream}, and a narrow bar means $\beta$ is
\emph{identifiable}, not that any temperature fits well. Second, the interval in
(iii) widens exactly when peaked logits make $\beta$ unidentifiable
($\mathrm{Var}_{q}[\ell_t]\to 0$), so forecasting regret and estimation precision
degrade in opposite directions. Third, the guarantee attaches to the mixture, not
a point estimator (proper online predictors face an exp-concavity obstruction
that improper mixtures avoid \citep{foster2018logistic}), so transferring it to
the fast filter (Section~\ref{sec:filter}) is an empirical, pre-registered claim.

\paragraph{Pre-registered validation.}
We committed four hypotheses, with sample sizes and thresholds, \emph{before}
generating any data (\texttt{paper/grid\_certificate\_prereg.md}); the bar uses
$c=\ln G$ on a $121$-bucket grid spanning $\log\beta\in[-3,3]$.
Table~\ref{tab:grid_certificate} reports the outcome. The bar covered the
continuum MLE on every well-specified synthetic stream (\gridCertCoverage); on
real Qwen-2.5-3B streams at $\beta{=}1$ the median width was
\gridCertMedianWidthBtOne{} in $\log\beta$ (the grid-resolution floor --- the bar
is far from vacuous where logits carry temperature information) and widened on
peaked-logit streams exactly as (iii) predicts. The transfer check to the Beat-2
filter passed (\gridCertFaithfulAgree{} of estimates inside the bar), while the
original $\beta{=}1$-linearized filter landed inside on only
\gridCertOriginalAgree{} --- so the certificate extends to the corrected filter's
outputs, not the original's.

\section{Generating better, and limitations}\label{sec:gen}
% =====================================================================
\paragraph{Can we use the read-out to \emph{generate} better? Not yet.}
The tempting move is to feed $\hat\beta_t$ back into the sampler to ``correct''
the temperature as it generates. It self-neutralizes: correcting toward
$\hat\beta$ drives the effective decoding temperature back to
$\beta_{\mathrm{dec}}\to 1$, quietly cancelling the very signal it reads off. We
confirmed this across exact-grid filters, so it is not an approximation artifact
(Appendix~\ref{app:controls}). Turning the read-out into better generation is the
honest open problem.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
These are preliminary steps, on a single model and tokenizer; the certificate
certifies the effective $\beta$ of the \emph{observed} stream, never a
counterparty's dial. The natural next step, for which it is the right tool, is
misspecification: the \emph{effective} temperature and goodness-of-fit that
top-$k$ and blacklist decoders register, where no single temperature reproduces
the stream and the best-fit bucket is a projection (synthetic pilot:
\texttt{scripts/e1\_synthetic\_pilot.py}).

\section{Corrector and supporting control experiments}\label{app:controls}
% =====================================================================
A corrector $\beta^{\mathrm{dec}}_t = \beta^{\mathrm{target}}/\hat\beta_t^{\,p}$
divides a user's target by the estimate. An empirical sweep shows it pins the
effective temperature in the ``boredom'' regime ($\beta^{\mathrm{target}}>1$) but
overshoots in the ``confusion'' regime ($\beta^{\mathrm{target}}<1$), where
low-$\beta$ self-generation degrades into incoherent text within $\sim$20 tokens
(a garbage-in/garbage-out confound that limits what the confusion-regime numbers
can claim). Two further controls are reported in full in the longer draft
(\texttt{paper/decoding\_paper.tex}): a sampler-switch experiment indicating the
belief is not a fast per-token entropy read-out (though entropy and rank
statistics are closely related, so this is a null on one specific mechanism, not
a positive identification), and a closed-loop-vs-invariance comparison whose only
firmly supported conclusion is ``do not correct at $\beta^{\mathrm{target}}=1$,
because Qwen is already calibrated there.''
